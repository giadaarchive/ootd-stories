# Authentication Appendix

A personal guide to authenticating fashion items — built from accumulated experience, research, and pattern recognition from handling real pieces. The goal is to distinguish genuine from fake, and vintage from reproduction, with enough confidence to buy, sell, and pass down correctly.

This is a living document. It grows with every purchase, every mistake, and every expert consulted.

---

## How this guide is structured

Each brand section covers:
- **Brand context** — what makes this brand's authentication complex
- **By category** — shoes, bags, scarves, clothing each have different tells
- **Authentic markers** — what genuine pieces consistently show
- **Fake markers** — what fakes consistently get wrong
- **Vintage markers** — how to date a piece and what vintage-specific authentication looks like

## Ferragamo (Salvatore Ferragamo)

**17 items in collection** — bags (2), shoes (10), scarves (1), clothing/knitwear (4)

**Brand context:** Founded in 1927 in Florence by Salvatore Ferragamo, shoemaker to the stars. Royal warrants from multiple royal families. The most commonly faked categories are shoes (especially the Vara pump) and silk scarves. Ferragamo has a long vintage history — pieces from the 1960s through 1990s are widely circulated and the label styles changed significantly across eras, making vintage dating important alongside authentication.

---

### Shoes

#### The Vara pump (signature model)

The Vara is the most recognisable Ferragamo shoe and the most faked. Authentication points:

**The bow:**
- Authentic: the grosgrain bow is flat, clean, and precisely folded. The ribbon weave is tight with no fraying at the fold.
- The metal Vara logo piece (small rectangular or oval plaque) sits at the centre of the bow on newer models. On vintage Vara, this piece may be absent or in a different position — not a fake indicator on its own.
- Fake: bow is floppy, asymmetrical, or poorly attached. Grosgrain looks thin or synthetic.

**The insole stamp:**
- Authentic: "SALVATORE FERRAGAMO" stamped in gold or silver foil inside the shoe, curved along the insole. Font is clean, evenly spaced, consistent depth.
- Below the name: "MADE IN ITALY" and the shoe size with width designation (e.g. "6½ B" — Ferragamo uses letters A through EEE for width).
- Fake: stamping is shallow, uneven spacing, wrong font weight, or missing the width designation entirely.

**The width system (important vintage tell):**
- Ferragamo is known for their width grading — this is a genuine differentiator. Authentic vintage shoes will have a letter width stamped. If a claimed vintage Vara has no width designation, question it.

**The sole:**
- Vintage Ferragamo: leather sole, usually with the brand name stamped on the heel cap.
- Modern Ferragamo: rubber or leather, with "FERRAGAMO" or "SF" on the heel.
- Fake: soles often feel light and hollow. Check the heel — authentic heels are solid and have consistent finish at the cap joint.

**The lining:**
- Authentic leather lining, consistent with the heel cup. The colour matches the upper or is a deliberate contrast.
- Fake: lining is often synthetic, peels at the edges, or has a different finish from the upper.

#### Other Ferragamo shoes (loafers, pumps, kitten heels)

Same stamping principles apply. Additionally:

- **Hardware** (buckles, bits): heavy and smooth on authentic pieces. Ferragamo gold-tone hardware has a consistent warm finish — not brassy or greenish. Check the back of the buckle — authentic buckles have clean finishing on the reverse side.
- **Gancini motif**: where present, the Gancini (horseshoe/hook shape) should be symmetrical and cleanly cast.

### Bags

#### The Gancini hardware

The Gancini clasp is Ferragamo's most distinctive bag element and its most significant authentication point.

- **Weight**: genuine Gancini hardware has noticeable weight. Pick up the clasp — it should feel substantial.
- **Finish**: smooth, consistent gold or silver tone. No rough casting marks on the visible face. The logo "SF" or Gancini shape is crisp, not blurred.
- **Movement**: the Gancini opens and closes with a satisfying click. Fakes often have loose, rattling hardware or a mechanism that doesn't close cleanly.

#### Interior label

- Authentic: "SALVATORE FERRAGAMO" in clean, evenly spaced lettering. "MADE IN ITALY" below.
- Serial or style number on a separate small label inside.
- Fake: label font is often slightly wrong — too bold, too thin, or with incorrect spacing. The stitching on the label itself may be uneven.

#### Leather and stitching

- Ferragamo's Vara bags (box leather, smooth calf): the leather has a slight structure — it does not collapse flat. Consistent grain, no patchwork appearance.
- Stitching: even tension throughout, consistent thread colour, corners turned cleanly. Examine the handle attachment points — this is where corners are cut on fakes.
- Lining: usually branded fabric lining with small "SF" or Gancini pattern. Authentic lining is taut and clean at the seams.

### Scarves

Ferragamo scarves are silk twill. Authentication:

**The silk test:**
- Hold the scarf up to light — silk twill has a characteristic weight and opacity. It drapes with intention, not loosely.
- Rub a corner quickly between fingers — real silk warms up. Polyester stays neutral or cool.
- Burn test (for loose threads only): real silk burns slowly with a self-extinguishing char and smells like burning hair. Polyester melts.

**The print:**
- "SALVATORE FERRAGAMO" or "FERRAGAMO" printed clearly in the border area, usually along one edge.
- Print registration: where colours meet, the boundary should be crisp. Misaligned colour layers are a fake indicator (authentic screen printing is precise).

**The hem:**
- Authentic Ferragamo scarves have a hand-rolled hem — the edge is folded over twice and hand-stitched in a consistent pattern. The stitching is slightly irregular (it's handmade) but the roll itself is even.
- Fake: machined hem, flat-stitched hem, or a hand-rolled appearance that's too perfect (usually machine-made to imitate).

### Clothing and knitwear

**Labels by era:**

| Era | Label style |
|-----|-------------|
| 1960s–1970s | "Salvatore Ferragamo" script font, usually gold on black or navy woven label |
| 1980s | "SALVATORE FERRAGAMO" block letters, serif font, "MADE IN ITALY" underneath |
| 1990s | Similar to 1980s, sometimes with country-specific distribution label added |
| 2000s onward | "FERRAGAMO" alone in sans-serif, with Gancini logo element on some pieces |

**Vintage clothing tells:**
- Pre-1990s Ferragamo ready-to-wear is rarer than shoes and bags. Authentic pieces from this era often have additional country-of-retail labels (e.g. "Exclusively for [retailer]") sewn in separately.
- Construction is visibly higher quality in vintage pieces — full lining, French seams, hand-finished buttonholes.
- The Vara bow motif appears on buttons, prints, and jacquard weaves in authentic vintage RTW.

**Knitwear:**
- Cashmere or wool-cashmere blend pieces should have a label indicating the fibre content.
- Feel test: fine-gauge Ferragamo knitwear is lightweight with no pilling on new pieces. Pilling on an unworn piece suggests lower quality or substitute fibre.

### Vintage Ferragamo — dating guide

Knowing the era helps authenticate, because the tells change.

| Decade | Key markers |
|--------|-------------|
| 1950s–1960s | Hand-lasted construction on shoes, wooden lasts visible in shoe shape. Unusual materials common (raffia, cork, fabric). Full name "Salvatore Ferragamo" always used. |
| 1970s | Platform era — wedges and platforms. Leather still dominant. Labels shift to simpler block-text format. |
| 1980s | Vara pump introduced (~1978, popularised through the 1980s). Block logo labels. Wider colour range in shoes. |
| 1990s | Brand expands into RTW. "FERRAGAMO" alone begins to appear alongside full name. Gancini hardware standardised. |
| 2000s onward | Modern branding. Gancini everywhere. Logo-forward. Higher faking frequency begins in this era. |

## Authentication methodology (general principles)

These apply across brands:

1. **Handle first, research second.** Spend time with authentic pieces before evaluating a purchase. Hands learn things eyes don't.
2. **Check where fakes cut corners.** The interior of zips, the back of clasps, the hem of scarves — these are expensive to fake correctly.
3. **Understand the era.** A 1970s piece will not have the same labels as a 1990s piece. Dating comes first; then authenticate against that era's standards.
4. **Know the weight.** Authentic hardware, leather, and silk all have characteristic weight. Fakes are often lighter because cheaper materials are lighter.
5. **Research the specific model.** The Vara has different tells from a loafer. A Gancini bag has different tells from a Fiamma.
6. **Document your mistakes.** When you authenticate something incorrectly, record why — what was the misleading tell? This is how the knowledge compounds.
